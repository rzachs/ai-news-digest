Must Read

  • [The Verge] Anthropic has officially filed to go public — What happened: Anthropic submitted a confidential draft registration to the SEC, kicking off its IPO process at a valuation of ~$965 billion — the highest of any private startup. What this means for you: If you're using or evaluating Claude (Anthropic's AI), expect the company to lean harder into enterprise revenue and pricing as it prepares for public market scrutiny. Also worth watching: public filings will eventually reveal real revenue numbers and how dependent these AI companies actually are on a handful of big customers.

  • [The Verge] Gemini Spark is the most impressive and terrifying AI experience I've had yet — What happened: Google launched Gemini Spark, an always-on AI assistant that works in the background on multi-step tasks — even when you're not actively using it. A Verge reviewer called it genuinely impressive at planning (not just generic suggestions), but also unsettling in how much access it requires. A second hands-on review flags real concerns about privacy tradeoffs and cost. What this means for you: This is the first AI assistant that reviewers say actually delivers on the trip-planning promise that's been overhyped for years. But "always on" and "works in the background" means giving Google continuous access to your activity. Evaluate the privacy cost carefully before adopting it for your workflow or recommending it to your team.

  • [The Verge] Meta's own AI was exploited to hijack Instagram accounts — What happened: Hackers used Meta's AI support chatbot to take over Instagram accounts — including one belonging to Barack Obama's White House profile — by tricking it into changing account credentials. Meta says it's been patched. What this means for you: This is a wake-up call for anyone deploying AI in customer-facing support roles. An AI that can take actions on accounts (change emails, reset passwords) needs stricter identity verification before acting. If your team is building or using AI-powered support tools, audit what actions they're authorized to take without human confirmation.

  • [Hugging Face Blog] Holo3.1: Fast & Local Computer Use Agents — What happened: H Company released Holo3.1, an open model that can control a computer — clicking, typing, navigating apps — running locally on your own machine rather than sending data to a cloud service. What this means for you: Local computer-control AI means no data leaves your machine, which is a meaningful advantage for privacy-sensitive workflows.

  • [Hugging Face Blog] Introducing Mellum2: A 12B Mixture-of-Experts Model by JetBrains — What happened: JetBrains (makers of developer tools like IntelliJ and PyCharm) released Mellum2, a free, open coding AI model designed to run efficiently by only activating the parts of the model needed for a given task — meaning it's fast without requiring expensive hardware. What this means for you: If your team uses JetBrains IDEs, this is a free, self-hostable coding assistant worth testing as an alternative to paying for GitHub Copilot or similar tools.

Trend to Watch

The biggest pattern today: AI is moving from "assistant you talk to" to "agent that acts on your behalf" — and the backlash and risks are arriving at the same speed as the capability. Google's Spark runs tasks in the background. Holo3.1 controls your computer. Meta's chatbot changed account credentials without proper verification. JetBrains ships a coding model that works inside your tools. These aren't chatbots — they're systems that do things, which means the privacy, security, and trust questions are now urgent, not theoretical. This week, when evaluating any new AI tool, ask one specific question before adopting it: "What actions can this AI take without me explicitly approving each one?" — and treat the answer as your primary adoption criterion.
