  • [The Verge] Read this before you vibe-code another app — What happened: A project manager built and launched a public website using AI-assisted "vibe coding" (building apps by describing what you want in plain English, letting AI write the code), only to discover months later it had a serious security vulnerability that could have exposed user data to attackers. What this means for you: If anyone on your team — or you yourself — is shipping AI-generated code directly to users without a security review, stop. AI writes plausible-looking code that can have dangerous blind spots. Before anything goes live, have someone specifically check for security issues, even if that means hiring a freelancer for a one-time audit. This isn't about slowing down; it's about not creating liability.

Trend to Watch

Enterprise AI is crossing a threshold — from cautious experimentation to full organizational deployment. Samsung's rollout is the loudest signal yet, but it reflects a broader shift: large companies are standardizing AI tools the way they once standardized email or Slack. The security story from The Verge is the necessary counterweight — speed of adoption is creating real exposure for teams shipping AI-generated work without proper review. These two stories are connected: as AI use scales up inside organizations, so does the surface area for mistakes.

This week, watch for whether your own organization has any clear policy on what gets reviewed before AI-generated work — code, content, or otherwise — goes live or reaches customers. If the answer is "we don't really have one," that's the gap worth closing now.
