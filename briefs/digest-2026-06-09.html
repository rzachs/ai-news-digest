Top Story

OpenAI has filed confidentially with the SEC to go public — the same move rival Anthropic made just a week ago. Both of the most important AI companies in the world are now on the IPO track simultaneously. The AI industry is entering a new financial era, and how these companies perform as public companies will reshape everything from pricing to product strategy.

Must Read

  • [The Verge] OpenAI Files for IPO, Following Anthropic — What happened: OpenAI submitted a confidential IPO filing to the SEC, one week after Anthropic did the same. Details like financials and risks aren't public yet, but the direction is clear. Anthropic is currently valued at roughly $61B. What this means for you: Both companies will soon face public-market pressure to grow revenue and cut costs. Expect pricing changes, faster feature shipping, and possibly more enterprise focus as they compete for Wall Street's attention. If you're building on either platform, now is the time to think about pricing lock-ins and vendor dependency.

  • [The Verge] Apple's AI Pitch Will Live or Die by Its Privacy Promise — What happened: Apple's WWDC was dominated by AI — a revamped Siri with a dedicated chat app, AI-powered photo and camera tools, and new automation features across iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, and Vision Pro. Apple's differentiator: privacy. They claim their cloud AI processing is as private as on-device, even though it now runs partly on Google's servers. What this means for you: If your team handles sensitive information, Apple's privacy-first framing matters — but the Google server revelation is a real tension worth watching. For most users, Apple is still catching up to ChatGPT and Claude in raw capability. Don't abandon your current AI tools, but do pay attention to how deeply AI gets baked into the devices your team already uses every day.

  • [The Verge] Apple is Using AI to Fix Safari's Extension Problem — What happened: Apple now lets Safari users describe a browser extension they want in plain English, and Safari will build it automatically. You describe what you need ("save cooking recipes and add notes"), and it generates a working extension. What this means for you: This is a preview of a bigger shift — describing what you want software to do instead of finding or buying it. If this works reliably, it changes how teams think about small workflow tools. Worth testing when iPadOS 26 ships publicly.

Trend to Watch

Two of the biggest stories today — Apple's WWDC and the OpenAI/Anthropic IPO filings — point to the same underlying shift: AI is moving from experimental to infrastructural. Apple is baking AI into every device your team already carries. OpenAI and Anthropic are preparing to answer to public shareholders. Both developments mean AI tools will face more scrutiny, more pricing pressure, and more competition for your attention and budget simultaneously. At the same time, Apple's "describe it, build it" approach to Safari extensions and Shortcuts hints at a near future where non-technical people create their own lightweight software tools on demand. This week, watch for early reviews of the iPadOS 26 developer beta — specifically whether Apple's plain-language automation tools actually work reliably in practice, because if they do, it's the most accessible AI capability Apple has ever shipped.
